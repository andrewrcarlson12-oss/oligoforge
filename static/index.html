In-silico PCR

BLASTs both primers and reports predicted products: same subject, opposite strands, convergent, in size range. The intended target should be the only product at the expected size. Remote BLAST is queued at NCBI (slow); local needs BLAST+ and a genome DB.

Isolate panel — inclusivity / exclusivity

In-silico PCR of one assay against a panel of real isolate sequences pulled from NCBI. Target isolates should amplify (sensitivity); near-neighbours should not (cross-reactivity). A screen, not a wet-lab result — but per strain it reports whether the pair makes the expected amplicon, the probe binds, and how clean the primer match and 3′-end are. Search a taxon for whole genomes (e.g. Salmonella enterica), or add a marker word to pull short single-gene records instead — Plasmodium cytb or Haemoproteus cytb returns one cytb per isolate, which is what a haemosporidian lineage panel needs. The picker lists every record's title and length, so you confirm exactly what goes in. Sequences are fetched and scanned one at a time.

Relative expression (ΔΔCq)

Normalised fold change from a finished run. Livak 2^−ΔΔCq, and — if you give per-assay efficiencies from your standard curves — the efficiency-corrected Pfaffl ratio. Multiple reference genes are combined by geometric mean (Vandesompele). Assumes good technical/biological replicates and a validated reference panel; it computes what the Cq values imply, with the assumptions stated.

How to use OligoForge — a short, accurate guide

OligoForge is an in-silico design + QC + reporting pipeline. It does not replace a calibrated vendor tool (confirm final oligos in IDT OligoAnalyzer) or the bench (every assay still needs wet-lab validation). Everything below is standard qPCR practice — IDT design guidance and Bustin et al., the MIQE guidelines — and the numbers match the thresholds this app actually uses.

The workflow

Target → assay (or Design) builds a primer/probe set on a sequence. Add it to the Workbench — that is the hub. From each assay card you run QC, in-silico PCR, the intron/gDNA check, conservation, a standard curve, generate the gBlock, and export an order, and each result saves onto that specific assay. Finish with the MIQE report, which fills its checklist from those saved results.

What design aims for (hydrolysis-probe assays)

Primers. 18–24 nt, Tm 59–64 °C, GC 35–65%, and the two primer Tms within about 2 °C of each other.

Amplicon. 70–150 bp — short products amplify efficiently and tolerate degraded template.

Probe. Tm roughly 8–10 °C above the primers, no 5′ G (it quenches the reporter), more C than G, and no run of four or more G's.

3′ ends. Avoid a strong GC clamp (no more than 3 G/C in the last five bases) and avoid a 3′ T where you can.

SYBR (no probe): primer-dimers decide everything, so F×R, F-self and R-self must all clear the dimer floor, and you confirm specificity with a melt curve showing a single peak.

Cycling — a starting point

Probe assays: 95 °C for 10 min, then 40–45 cycles of (95 °C 15 s → 60 °C 60 s), reading at 60 °C. SYBR: three-step with the anneal near (lowest primer Tm − 4 °C), followed by a melt curve. Optimize the annealing temperature on your own instrument; a gradient is ideal, and MIQE expects you to report it.

Validating an assay (so the report's boxes tick)

Specificity. Run in-silico PCR (and/or BLAST). One predicted product of the expected size is the goal; confirm it empirically by gel, melt, or sequencing.

gDNA / intron. Design across an exon-exon junction, or DNase-treat and run a no-RT control. The automated intron check needs NCBI exon annotation, which many non-model organisms simply do not have — when it can't resolve, verify empirically and use "mark handled" on the assay card, which the report records as user-asserted rather than computed.

Efficiency, R², LOD. Run a standard curve over a dilution series (Std curve tab) and click "attach to current assay." MIQE acceptance: efficiency 90–110% (slope between −3.1 and −3.58), R² ≥ 0.98. A formal limit of detection needs roughly 60 replicates near the low end.

Always. Include a no-template control; for RT-qPCR, a no-RT control and a note on RNA integrity.

A worked pass (the loaded FSJ panel)

Use "load example (FSJ)" in the Workbench to get a TaqMan panel. For each assay: QC primers → Specificity (in-silico PCR) → set the gene and organism and try the intron check → run a standard curve from your Cq data and attach it → generate the gBlock as your quantification standard → export the IDT order → generate the MIQE report.

Honest limits

Tm and secondary-structure numbers are real engine values at your reaction salt; displayed Tm uses IDT OligoAnalyzer’s SantaLucia 1998 + Owczarzy 2008 nearest-neighbor method, and LNA Tm is a McTigue-informed point estimate (~±2 °C) — confirm it in OligoAnalyzer Affinity Plus. In-silico PCR is a screen, not a guarantee; NCBI Primer-BLAST is the more authoritative specificity check. Nothing here is wet-lab validated. Treat OligoForge as a fast first pass and a record-keeper, not a substitute for validation.

Reset to factory settings
